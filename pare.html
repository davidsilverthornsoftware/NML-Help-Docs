 Issue Resolution Process

There are three major stages in the Issue Resolution Process. The first stage is identifying the issue. The second stage is performing the actual steps of the resolution. The third and final step is communicating with the customer to ensure the issue is resolved. To illustrate this we have developed the P.A.R.E.         Process.

The P. A. R. E. Process.

PARE stands for:

P = Probe – Gather Information about the issue. Ask questions and get as much data as possible.

A = Analyze – Verify the issue, attempt to recreate the issue if appropriate, use appropriate diagnostics and resources to research a resolution.

  • Technical Requirements Met
  • Internet Connection Speed
  • Replicate in multiple browsers
  • Refresh and/or Reset their browser
  • Get a Screenshot
  • Get a System Analysis
  • Review help documentation, both internal and external
  • Use Google and troubleshoot, troubleshoot, troubleshoot

R = Resolve – Work with the customer to resolve the resolution directly. Walk them through the process of making changes or running utilities to resolve the issue they are experiencing. If you are unable to resolve the issue, after trying all of the available analyze steps, then escalate it to a higher tier.

E = Educate – Inform the customer of what occurred and provide them with information that will help them avoid this issue in the future. Show them how to resolve the issue and confirm issue resolution.

To better understand the troubleshooting and issue resolution process read below.

Gather Information

It is important to know as much as you can about the situation before you jump headlong into trying to fix it. Gathering information is the first step in successful troubleshooting.

If the customer is available or able to explain the nature of the situation. In those cases, the following tips will assist you in getting accurate and useful information from your customer.

Ask Probing Questions

When you question a customer, it is very important to understand that the customer is generally not happy with his or her situation. Your courtesy and professionalism will make the circumstances better for the customer and enable you to gather information needed to resolve the issue. Be patient. Be polite. Be constantly aware that you are there to help the customer.

Furthermore, be aware that a customer coming to you for help with a situation probably will not share your level of technical expertise and may therefore use incorrect or imprecise terminology. Try to talk to customers at their level rather than attempt to impress them with your knowledge and mastery of all things technical.

The following are tips you should follow when gathering information from customers:

  1. Start with open questions such as “What is the issue?” Open questions generally start with words like how, why, when, who, what, and where. They cannot be answered with “yes” or “no.”
  2. Let customers explain in their own words what they have experienced. Do not interrupt the customer—interrupting generally prompts someone to start over.
  3. As you begin to understand the basics of the issue, start using closed questions that require more limited, specific answers. “What operating system are you using?” is an example of a closed question. The customer will either tell you what the OS version is or tell you that he or she does not know. Closed questions often can be answered with “yes” or “no.”
  4. Verify your understanding of what the customer has told you. Restate what you have been told and get the customer's agreement that you understand the issue. An example of restatement would be, “Okay, so what's happening is that when you try X, Y happens. Is that correct?”
  5. If the customer agrees that you understand, continue to gather information. If the customer does not agree that you understand, clarify what you misstated and again verify your understanding. Do not continue until the customer agrees that you understand the issue.

Analyze and Verify the Issue

Verifying the issue is extremely important in successful troubleshooting. It gives you a chance to objectively confirm the extent and the nature of the situation. In the long run, it saves time since you do not waste time working on the wrong issue.

Attempt to Recreate the Issue

Using the information you have gathered, setup the system and try to recreate the issue.

For every issue, there can be other explanations for the system's behavior. Make sure you aren't trying to troubleshoot a nonexistent issue or an issue that has not been well defined. Attempting the task yourself, and asking for screenshots from a customer, can assist you in recreating the issue and making observations that will be helpful in troubleshooting.

Step Back and Ask More Questions

Before you continue to troubleshoot, ask your customer (or yourself, if the customer is not available) the following questions:

  • Can you reproduce the situation now?

Intermittent issues can be very hard to isolate. Probe for circumstances or environmental factors that may be significant.

  • Do they meet the system requirements?

There may be specific issues with the system specifications of the computer that the customer is using.

  • When did the issue start? Have they been able to use this product previously? What was the last thing changed or added to the system?

With these questions, you can determine whether the introduction of new hardware or software may have contributed to, or caused, the issue.

  • How is this feature intended to work?

Here you can determine whether the customer is confused or misinformed about a feature's proper function.

  • Does the issue occur only with one specific application?

In this case, you are trying to determine whether the issue is systemic or affects only one program.

  • What other software and devices are used with this system?

Understanding the system's operating environment can provide you with helpful indications of where the issue may be based. A System Analysis is useful in this process.

  • What has the customer done to try to resolve the issue?

The customer's actions may give you insight into the customer's technical expertise and may answer some of the other questions.

Resolve The Issue

A quick fix is not necessarily the most likely solution to the issue, but because it is easy to perform and involves little time or expense, it is worth trying. There is nothing more frustrating than spending hours isolating an issue only to find out later that a quick fix solves it.

A quick fix is defined here as a repair action that:

  • Can be performed quickly
  • Involves little or no risk of harm to the system
  • Has little or no cost

An experienced, efficient troubleshooter will try one or more quick fixes before taking on the more time-consuming tasks involved with isolating the issue. If the issue is not resolved after you try these quick fixes, you can confidently move to the more time-consuming task of logically and methodically isolating the issue.

Here are some more examples of quick fixes:

  • Have the customer run the File Association utility.
  • Clear the browser cache and/or reset the browser.
  • Shutdown and restart the computer.
  • Have the customer complete an online repair of MS Office.

This is only a partial list. The situation and your experience will determine which quick fixes make sense for troubleshooting the issue you are working on. Make sure you understand the issue and the resolution before providing a fix. The only thing more frustrating than spending time researching a simple issue is providing unnecessary and inappropriate “quick fixes” and not resolving an issue.

As you gain experience, you will develop your own collection of quick fixes. The following section lists other quick fixes that might be appropriate. Some of them can affect data on the customer's system, so you will have to consult with the customer to determine whether he or she has a current backup and weigh the advantages of the quick fix against the possible inconvenience or time required.

Quick Fixes for Technical Support

Level 1: Innocuous/No Impact

  • Restart or shutdown.
  • Start up in Safe Mode which loads only the minimum necessary files and performs an elaborate directory check of the hard disk (which is why it can take a long time to start up). After the system is powered on have the customer press F8 repeatedly until the boot menu appears. Have them then select Safe Mode from the menu and continue the boot process. To end the safe boot and get back to typical operation, just restart as normal.
  • Repair or reset the web browser.
  • Complete system updates or other updates such as Adobe Flash.
  • Install another web browser.
  • Repair Microsoft Office.

Level 2: Moderate Impact

  • Adjust user account settings.
  • If troubleshooting a network issue, check the settings in the Firewall tab in Sharing preferences.
  • Force quit a troublesome application by holding down the Ctrl, Alt and Delete keys. Right clicking the program that is experiencing an issue and selecting End Process.
  • Log in as a (new) user in Accounts preferences. Since most user settings are tied to the user account, you can create a new account with which you can test a more standardized user environment, presumably with no conflicting or corrupted system resources.
  • Move, rename, or delete potentially problematic preferences files or folders. The applications that use the preference files will automatically re-create clean copies as necessary.
  • Reinstall the suspect application.

Level 3: Invasive/High Impact

  • Any higher level fixes or resolutions need to be recommended and/or approved by a senior technician. These issues are commonly escalated.

Use Additional Resources to Research the Issue

At the start of this document you learned that, along with good troubleshooting technique, product knowledge and experience are the basis for efficient, professional troubleshooting. If you have completed the steps described so far and still can't determine the source of the issue, it is time to research additional resources.

In situations in which you may not have in-depth experience or product knowledge, you can use such references as the Forum and FileMaker Ticket Database.

Escalate the Issue

If you still cannot troubleshoot an issue despite your best efforts, you may need to escalate your issue to Tier 2.

Verify the Resolution

Verifying the success of the resolution directly or by follow up communication will ensure that the customer has been helped completely. Please remember to verify the following before closing a Ticket.

  • The entire issue has been resolved.
  • No new issues have been introduced during troubleshooting and resolution.

Educating the Customer

Once you have resolved the issue for normal customer use (or escalated the issue), inform the customer of the work that you completed and help them to understand the issue. This will keep your customers informed and help them to avoid this issue in the future. Additionally, this will give the customer greater faith in the support they have received.

The basic idea is to give customers information to improve their computing experience. Taking time to teach customers how to avoid future issues adds value and improves their experience.

Please note that, while in some cases customers will not want to engage in the why of an issue, most customers will respond positively to being educated about the issue they experienced.

Complete Documentation

As a Technical Support Specialist you have certain procedures for documenting service and support. These include not only ticketing of the issue but may also involve writing forum posts about the issue to help other technicians in the future. Make sure that you take all necessary steps in documentation. This will help to provide the highest quality service to all of our customers.
